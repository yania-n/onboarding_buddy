3. The TCP Team Role — What You Do
The Technical Coordination & Planning team has a distinct, strategic character. It is not a traditional delivery team and it is not a business team. It sits in between — accountable for ensuring that technical work is well-planned, well-governed, and well-communicated. Here is what each role brings:
Core Responsibilities
Backlog ownership
You own and maintain your squad's product backlog end-to-end. This means creating, refining, prioritising, and grooming user stories and tasks, and ensuring the backlog is always healthy enough to sustain sprint planning at least two sprints ahead.
Requirements translation
You receive business requirements (epics, user needs, capability requests) from the Functional PO and transform them into user stories the development team can work from. This includes writing acceptance criteria, defining edge cases, and documenting constraints.
Technical bridge
You attend architecture discussions, technical design sessions, and developer walkthroughs. You don't need to write code, but you must understand technical trade-offs well enough to make informed prioritisation decisions and communicate them back to business stakeholders.
Sprint facilitation
You lead or co-lead sprint planning, backlog refinement, sprint review, and retrospective ceremonies for your squad. You are responsible for ensuring stories are 'ready' before they enter a sprint, and 'done' by the end of it.
Stakeholder communication
You keep the Stakeholder Team informed of sprint progress, blockers, and delivery changes. You escalate when needed, and you shield the development team from scope creep and unplanned interruptions.
What You Are NOT Responsible For
Writing code or conducting technical design (that's the Technical Team)
Defining top-level business strategy or KPIs (that's the FPO and Business Consultants)
UAT sign-off on behalf of the business (that's the FPO or business stakeholders)
Line managing developers or tech leads

4. How We Work — Scrum & Sprints
The Analytics Platform team operates using Scrum methodology with 2-week sprints. All squads run on the same sprint cadence — sprints begin on Monday and end on the Friday of the following week.
Sprint Calendar
The Sprint in Detail
Sprint Planning (Day 1, Monday morning, ~2 hours)
The squad meets to commit to the sprint backlog. As TPO, you present the prioritised stories, confirm they meet the Definition of Ready, and facilitate the team's capacity and commitment discussion. Output: a sprint goal and a committed sprint backlog.
Daily Stand-up (Days 1–10, every morning, 15 minutes)
Each team member answers three questions: What did I do yesterday? What will I do today? Any blockers? As TPO, you attend, listen actively for blockers you can unblock, and flag stories at risk. You do not need to speak unless you have an update relevant to the team.
Backlog Refinement (Mid-sprint, typically Wednesday Week 1, ~90 minutes)
This is your most important recurring ceremony. You lead the refinement of upcoming stories with the development team — walking through acceptance criteria, estimating with story points (modified Fibonacci: 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13), and flagging dependencies or technical risks. Target: next sprint's backlog is fully groomed and 'ready' by end of this session.
Sprint Review (Day 10, Friday, ~45 minutes)
The squad demonstrates completed work to stakeholders and the Functional PO. As TPO, you co-facilitate with the Dev Lead. Focus is on demonstrating working software against the sprint goal. Incomplete stories are not shown — they are returned to the backlog.
Sprint Retrospective (Day 10, Friday, ~60 minutes, immediately after review)
A private squad conversation about how we worked, not what we built. Typical format: Start / Stop / Continue. As TPO, you facilitate the retro. Action items are owned, dated, and reviewed at the next retro.
5. Requirements Flow & Collaboration Model
How Requirements Travel
Requirements flow from business need to delivered feature through a structured, multi-stage process. The TCP team sits at the critical translation point in this chain.
Working with the Functional PO
The Functional PO (FPO) is your primary business counterpart. They own the product vision and prioritisation at the feature/epic level. You own the backlog and story level below that.
Meet with your FPO weekly (minimum) to review the product roadmap and upcoming epics
Challenge requirements that lack clarity or are technically unachievable — this is expected and valued
Surface technical constraints early rather than absorbing them silently
Align on sprint scope before Sprint Planning so there are no surprises in the ceremony
Working with the Development Team
You are the development team's first point of contact for anything product or requirements related. Your job is to give them clarity so they can focus on building.
Never skip refinement — stories that aren't groomed will block the sprint
Developers will raise technical questions and edge cases; you answer or find the answer quickly
Respect the team's capacity estimates — don't overload the sprint based on stakeholder pressure
Shield the team from unplanned work during the sprint; negotiate scope changes through you

7. Backlog Management & Prioritisation
Backlog Structure
Each squad maintains a single Jira project with the following hierarchy:
Epic — a large body of work representing a feature or capability area (owned by FPO)
Story — a user-facing unit of value, broken from an epic (owned by TPO; technically reviewed by Architect)
Subtask — technical breakdown within a story (owned by Developer)
Bug — a defect in existing functionality (triaged by TPO; technical severity assessed by Architect or Developer)
Spike — a time-boxed research task with a fixed output (e.g. 2-day investigation)

Backlog Health Rules
The backlog must always have a minimum of 2 fully groomed sprints ready ahead
Stories older than 90 days without a sprint assignment must be reviewed and either re-prioritised or archived
No story enters a sprint without meeting the Definition of Ready (see Section 8)
Story points must be estimated collaboratively — never estimated by the TPO alone; the Architect and Developer on the TCP team should contribute to technical estimates
8. Definition of Ready & Definition of Done
Definition of Ready (DoR)
A story is 'Ready' when it meets ALL of the following criteria:
User story is written in the format: As a [user type], I want [action] so that [benefit]
Acceptance criteria are written and agreed with the FPO
All dependencies are identified and either resolved or explicitly accepted
The story has been estimated in story points by the development team
Any required wireframes, data models, or design specs are linked in the Jira ticket
The story fits within a single sprint (if not, it must be split)
Technical approach has been discussed with at least one developer or tech lead
Definition of Done (DoD)
A story is 'Done' when it meets ALL of the following criteria:
All acceptance criteria have been met and verified
Code has been reviewed and merged to the main branch
Unit and integration tests are written and passing
The feature has been deployed to the staging environment
The FPO or a designated business stakeholder has reviewed the output
Documentation (if required) is updated in Confluence
The Jira ticket is moved to 'Done' and release notes are updated
Both the DoR and the DoD are reviewed and updated at the beginning of each quarter with input from the whole squad.
9. Communication Norms
Channels & Purposes
Response Time Expectations
Written Communication Standards
Much of the TCP team's communication is written — in Jira, Confluence, and Slack. Some principles we hold to:
Be specific, not vague. 'The feature should handle large datasets' is not a requirement. 'The feature must load a dataset of up to 10M rows in under 3 seconds' is.
Separate facts from opinions when communicating with the development team. Own your decisions; label your assumptions.
When in doubt, over-document. A well-written decision log in Confluence is worth hours of later confusion.
Avoid jargon with business stakeholders; avoid business-only language with developers. Code-switch deliberately.
